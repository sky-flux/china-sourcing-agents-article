OBD2 (On-Board Diagnostics) scanners are software-as-much-as-hardware products. When sourcing automotive electronics from China, the internal PCBA chipset determines which vehicle makes and OBD-II protocols are supported, while the paired mobile app determines the end-user experience.

**Genuine Chipset vs. ELM327 Clones.** Most budget wholesale OBD2 adapters use cheap ELM327 clone chips (often misleadingly marked as "ELM327 v1.5" or "v2.1" — neither version actually exists; the final genuine ELM327 is v1.4b). Cheap clones suffer from incomplete protocol support: they frequently fail on CAN-based systems from Toyota, GM, and Ford. Always ask the Chinese factory specifically which microcontroller/chipset is used (e.g., PIC18F25K80 is preferred for clones) and request a comprehensive vehicle compatibility list derived from real-world test data.

**Mobile App Ecosystem and OBD API Access.** OBD2 scan tools are only as useful as their companion diagnostic app. For private-label automotive products, clarify whether the OBD API is open (which supports popular third-party apps like Torque Pro, Car Scanner ELM OBD2, and OBD Auto Doctor) or locked to a proprietary, often poorly translated, factory app. Open APIs offer significantly higher customer perceived value for DIY mechanics and car enthusiasts.
