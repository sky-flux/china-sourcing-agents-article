A robot vacuum cleaner is an autonomous floor-care appliance that combines a suction motor, brush roll, navigation sensors, lithium battery, and microcontroller in a self-docking disc-shaped chassis. For OEM and private-label buyers, it sits at the intersection of smart home hardware and small-appliance manufacturing: the bill of materials spans motors, LiDAR or camera modules, cells, HEPA filtration, and optional app connectivity. Typical buyers are Amazon sellers launching a private-label line, European distributors replacing a retiring SKU, or smart-home brands adding cleaning robots to an existing ecosystem. Most sourcing starts in Shenzhen, Dongguan, and Foshan, where the Shenzhen electronics manufacturing corridor offers the densest concentration of motor, sensor, and plastic-injection suppliers.

LiDAR-based navigation uses a spinning time-of-flight sensor to build a real-time map — typically accurate to within 2–3 cm in flat environments. vSLAM (visual simultaneous localization and mapping) uses a downward or upward-facing camera plus accelerometer data. For smart home device sourcing, LiDAR is the preferred navigation technology for consistent performance in real-world conditions. In practice, LiDAR performs more consistently in low-light environments and returns to base reliably. vSLAM is cost-competitive ($8–12 less per unit) but struggles in rooms with repeating floor patterns.

For the EU market, verify the LiDAR sensor's laser class: Class 1 (eye-safe) is required for consumer products. Some lower-cost units use Class 2 sensors with a diffuser — this is not EN 60825-1 compliant. Request the laser classification documentation from the sensor supplier. Also verify brush roll motor current draw under load: a brush roll that stalls on thick carpet without triggering the overcurrent protection will burn out within the warranty period. When sourcing robot vacuums, include these stress tests in your sample evaluation checklist.
