Off-grid inverters carry safety-critical loads — medical equipment, refrigeration, lighting. Quality failures here are not just financial; they can be dangerous. When sourcing power electronics like inverters, safety certification must be the primary qualification filter.

**IEC 62109-1 vs. self-declared CE.** IEC 62109-1 is the safety standard for power converters in photovoltaic systems. A proper CE mark on an inverter should reference this standard in the Declaration of Conformity. Many budget inverters carry a CE mark that simply states "tested to EN 55032" (EMC) without the safety standard — this is technically valid but misrepresents compliance for PV use. A factory audit should verify the test reports behind all claimed certifications. Ask for the specific test report number and verify it against the certification body's database.

**Modified sine wave vs. pure sine wave.** Modified sine wave inverters will damage or reduce the life of many loads: variable-speed motor drives, medical devices, some lighting dimmers, and audio equipment. Specify pure sine wave output, and verify it with an oscilloscope on a sample unit — not just the specification sheet. This verification should be part of your quality inspection protocol.
