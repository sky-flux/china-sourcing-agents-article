When importing remote control toys, compliance is critical. EN 71 is the European toy safety standard — Part 1 covers mechanical and physical properties (sharp edges, projectile hazards, cord length), Part 2 covers flammability, and Part 3 covers chemical properties (migration of elements including lead, cadmium, chromium from paints and plastics). For RC vehicles classified as toys (marketed to the under-14 age group), all three parts are mandatory to obtain the CE marking. When sourcing RC cars from a China wholesale manufacturer, EN 71 compliance should be strictly verified before placing any bulk production order.

The most common failure point in EN 71 testing for RC cars is Part 3 chemical migration: decorative body shell paints and sticker adhesives are the primary risk. Premium paints from established suppliers (like Tamiya, RPM, or their qualified Chinese equivalents) feature EN 71-3 compliant formulas. However, generic hobby spray paints used by some lower-tier factories often fail barium and antimony migration limits. Always verify the paint supplier and request an EN 71-3 test report from an accredited lab (CNAS or ILAC accredited). Conducting a thorough factory audit should confirm the paint supply chain documentation to ensure your wholesale RC cars are completely safe for import.

The 2.4GHz radio transmitter controller included with every modern RC car requires FCC ID authorization for legal sale in the United States — operating a radio device without an FCC ID is a direct violation of FCC Part 15. FCC certification generally takes 6–10 weeks and costs between $2,000–$3,500. Reliable Chinese factories producing for the US market typically hold an FCC ID for their standard radio modules; buyers must verify the FCC ID appears on the transmitter housing and perfectly matches the FCC database entry (search fccid.io).
