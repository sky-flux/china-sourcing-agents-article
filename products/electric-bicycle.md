An OEM electric bicycle is a pedal-assisted bicycle built to a buyer’s frame, motor, and battery specification, then assembled and branded at a China factory. It combines a mechanical bicycle platform with a battery-motor drive system, so sourcing it successfully means controlling both frame quality and electrical safety. Most buyers order 50–200 units for a first run, with 45–70 day lead times depending on battery cell availability and custom paint or decal work. Voltage class, motor wattage, and speed limit must match the target market from the start — retrofitting compliance after tooling is expensive.

Before placing a purchase order, lock the spec sheet in writing. Confirm motor rated power (250W for EU EPAC, 500W for US Class 2), motor type (rear hub or mid-drive such as Bafang M200/M400), battery voltage and capacity (36V 10.4Ah or 48V 13Ah), cell brand (Samsung or LG), controller current limit, display type, charger safety approvals, frame alloy (6061-T6 aluminum), brake type (hydraulic disc), and wheel size. Also confirm that the supplier’s EN 15194, CE, UN 38.3, and RoHS documentation covers the exact SKU you will import, not just a generic platform.

The most expensive failure in custom e-bike sourcing is an undocumented battery change. Some factories quote Samsung or LG cells, then build production units with unbranded cells that lose capacity within 200 cycles and lack thermal runaway protection. Demand a cell declaration, cycle-test report (≥80% retention after 500 cycles), and BMS test data for overcharge, overdischarge, overcurrent, and balancing. This crosses into vehicle-grade electronics compliance territory, so treat weak documentation as a red flag and switch suppliers if the factory refuses an independent cell inspection.
