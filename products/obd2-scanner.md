An OBD2 Bluetooth scanner is a small plug-in adapter that reads diagnostic trouble codes (DTCs), live sensor data, and emissions readiness from a vehicle's On-Board Diagnostics port and streams the information to a phone or tablet. It is used by DIY mechanics, small repair shops, fleet operators, and aftermarket accessories brands that want a low-cost entry point into vehicle diagnostics without buying dedicated workshop hardware.

When sourcing automotive electronics from China, the internal PCBA chipset determines which vehicle makes and OBD-II protocols are supported, while the paired mobile app determines the end-user experience. Many factories in the Shenzhen electronics cluster produce these adapters, but component and firmware quality vary widely.

**Genuine Chipset vs. ELM327 Clones.** Most budget wholesale OBD2 adapters use cheap ELM327 clone chips (often misleadingly marked as "ELM327 v1.5" or "v2.1" — neither version actually exists; the final genuine ELM327 is v1.4b). Cheap clones suffer from incomplete protocol support: they frequently fail on CAN-based systems from Toyota, GM, and Ford. Always ask the Chinese factory specifically which microcontroller/chipset is used (e.g., PIC18F25K80 is preferred for clones) and request a comprehensive vehicle compatibility list derived from real-world test data.
