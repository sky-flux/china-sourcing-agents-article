An angle grinder is a handheld rotary power tool that drives an abrasive disc at high speed for cutting, grinding, and surface preparation. It is used daily in metal fabrication, automotive repair, masonry, and construction, and it is one of the most common items in a professional power tools catalog. The product has three core assemblies: an AC or brushless DC motor, a gearbox that reduces RPM while increasing torque, and a safety guard around the spinning disc. Because the tool runs at 11,000–12,000 RPM and handles high lateral loads, small weaknesses in the spindle, bearings, or guard become dangerous failures in the field.

Before you request samples from an angle grinder manufacturer in China, lock down the spec sheet. The disc size (115mm or 125mm) determines the depth of cut and the guard geometry. Motor power ratings of 850W, 950W, 1,200W, or 1,400W should match the duty cycle you advertise — a 950W motor sold as “professional” will overheat under continuous metal-shop use. Confirm the no-load speed (usually 11,000–12,000 RPM), the spindle thread (M14 for EU markets), and the vibration emission value measured under EN 60745-2-3. Protection class matters: IP20 is standard, but IP54 is worth specifying if the tool will be used on dusty construction sites. Weight should stay between 1.6kg and 2.2kg including the side handle, or user fatigue becomes a return driver.

CE marking is the legal minimum for EU market entry, but GS (Geprüfte Sicherheit) certification is a voluntary mark tested by accredited bodies such as TÜV, DEKRA, or VDE.
