EN 1888-1:2018 replaced the previous EN 1888:2012 standard for wheeled child conveyances and baby carriages. It became mandatory for European Union market compliance in 2020. The key regulatory changes include much more stringent stability requirements (such as the topple test with a full newborn load including a loaded parent bag hook), updated restraint system strength tests for the 5-point safety harness, and extensively revised braking requirements. Our factory audit service strictly verifies that the Chinese stroller manufacturer's certification document references EN 1888-1:2018 explicitly — baby strollers certified only under the older EN 1888:2012 do not meet current EU market requirements and risk customs rejection.

Brake mechanism retention is a critical infant safety function tested per EN 1888-1 Section 5.7: the parking brake must securely hold the loaded pram (with maximum child weight plus full shopping basket load) on a 10° slope. We test this physically during factory sample inspection — we park the stroller on a 10° incline with the appropriate test weight and verify it does not roll. Chinese OEM factories sometimes use cheap plastic brake clips that work correctly when new but experience material fatigue and lose retention force after 500 cycles of folding and daily use. Always specify a minimum brake retention force of 50 N in your wholesale purchase order. Our sourcing service can seamlessly include brake retention testing, folding mechanism durability checks, and frame weld inspection as part of the rigorous pre-production sample evaluation.

Lightweight aluminum frame welds on modern baby strollers are the primary structural concern for both safety and product longevity. TIG welds on the 6061 aluminum alloy should be visually inspected for undercut, porosity, and incomplete fusion — look particularly closely at the folding joint weld (the highest mechanical stress point) and the backrest recline mechanism mounting points. For EU-market baby strollers, EN 1888-1 requires a rigorous frame strength test: applying a 750 N horizontal load to the handlebar for 10 continuous minutes without causing permanent deformation. Always request this official test result from the stroller factory before mass manufacturing.
