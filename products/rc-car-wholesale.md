EN 71 is the European toy safety standard — Part 1 covers mechanical and physical properties (sharp edges, projectile hazards, cord length), Part 2 covers flammability, and Part 3 covers chemical properties (migration of elements including lead, cadmium, chromium from paints and plastics). For RC vehicles classified as toys (marketed to under-14 age group), all three parts are mandatory for CE marking. When sourcing RC cars from China, EN 71 compliance should be verified before placing a production order.

The most common failure point in EN 71 testing for RC cars is Part 3 chemical migration: decorative body shell paints and sticker adhesives are the primary risk. Paints from established suppliers (Tamiya, RPM, or their qualified Chinese equivalents) have EN 71-3 compliant formulas. Generic hobby spray paints used by some factories fail barium and antimony migration limits. Verify the paint supplier and request an EN 71-3 test report from an accredited lab (CNAS or ILAC accredited). A factory audit should confirm the paint supply chain documentation.

The 2.4GHz radio transmitter in every RC car requires FCC ID authorization for sale in the United States — operating a radio device without FCC ID is a violation of FCC Part 15. FCC certification takes 6–10 weeks and costs $2,000–3,500. Chinese factories producing for the US market typically hold FCC ID for their standard radio modules; verify the FCC ID appears on the transmitter housing and matches the FCC database entry (search fccid.io). See our CE and FCC certification guide for more on radio device testing requirements.
