USB-C is the most specification-abused connector class in the current market. The physical receptacle is standardized; the electrical implementation is not. A connector that passes visual inspection and basic continuity can still lack the CC resistors for PD negotiation, be rated for 3A when your design requires 5A, have gold plating below 0.2 µm that fails at 500 mating cycles instead of the specified 10,000, or have contact geometry out of spec causing intermittent connection in the field. Chinese production ranges from Amphenol ICC-manufactured parts with full traceability to unlabeled commodity connectors where the spec sheet is a photocopy of someone else's datasheet with the logo changed.

USB-C (IEC 62680-1-3) is a 24-pin connector used for both power and data. The same physical form factor carries USB 2.0 (480 Mb/s), USB 3.2 Gen 2×2 (20 Gb/s), USB4 (40 Gb/s), DisplayPort Alt Mode, Thunderbolt 4, and USB Power Delivery up to 240W EPR — still far below the kilowatt-class high-power charging connectors used in EV applications. The pinout includes four power pins (VBUS), four ground pins (GND), two CC (Configuration Channel) pins for orientation detection and PD negotiation, two SBU (Sideband Use) pins, and high-speed differential pairs for data.

Receptacle variants differ primarily by mounting style. Mid-mount receptacles sit partially recessed into the PCB, reducing overall height — standard in wearables and thin consumer devices. Top-mount (surface-mount) receptacles sit on the PCB surface and are easier to rework — the typical choice for the multiple downstream ports in USB-C hubs and docking products. Through-hole receptacles provide mechanical strength for applications with high mating stress (test equipment, industrial). Vertical vs. horizontal orientation affects the PCB routing path to the USB controller IC.
