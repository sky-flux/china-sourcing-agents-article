Wire standard selection is the first critical decision in any automotive wiring harness design, and it cannot be corrected cheaply after the cable assembly tooling is complete. The three dominant global standards — FLRY-B, GXL, and XLPE — differ significantly in insulation material, temperature ceiling, regulatory approval, and mass. Specifying the wrong wire type for your custom OEM wiring harness means either a finished harness that fails automotive-grade qualification testing or one that is overbuilt and overweight for its specific vehicle application.

**FLRY-B (ISO 6722, European OEM standard).** Single-core vehicle cable with thin-wall PVC insulation. Temperature rated to +105°C continuous, -40°C cold bend. Mass-optimized — at 1.5mm² cross-section, FLRY-B is approximately 15% lighter than GXL at the same conductor area, which matters immensely at scale when a typical passenger car carries 60–100m of electrical wiring. The thin-wall construction reduces outer diameter, which aids harness routing density in tight conduit bundles. Trade-off: PVC insulation is not cross-linked, so it softens under sustained heat above 105°C and is not rated for continuous oil immersion. Not UL-listed — making it unacceptable for North American OEM programs that require UL approval, but standard for European and Chinese domestic OEM supplier chains.

**GXL (SAE J1128, North American OEM standard).** Cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) insulation with a thin-wall profile. Temperature rated to +125°C continuous, offering excellent resistance to engine oils, automotive fuels, and coolants. UL-listed under UL 44. It is heavier than FLRY-B at an equivalent cross-section due to the denser XLPE compound. Specified by North American OEMs (GM, Ford, Chrysler) and their tier-1 harness suppliers as the default low-voltage (LV) wire. If your customer's sourcing specification references SAE J1128 or calls out GXL/TXL/SXL designations, FLRY-B is not a compliant substitute regardless of a similar physical appearance.
