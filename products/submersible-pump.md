The first decision is selecting the correct pump family. The three main types serve fundamentally different hydraulic and mechanical requirements, and swapping one for another after tooling or certification is expensive.

**Deep-well borehole pumps** have a long, slender cylindrical body designed to fit inside a cased well. The motor is at the bottom, the impeller stack is above it, and water enters through screen intakes along the body. These are multi-stage centrifugal designs — each stage adds roughly 10–40 m of head depending on impeller diameter and rotational speed. Typical applications: irrigation water supply for drip irrigation systems, municipal groundwater extraction, and ground-source heat pump circulation circuits. The borehole casing ID is the binding constraint: a **4-inch casing** (nominal 100 mm ID) accepts a pump body with a **99 mm maximum OD**; a 6-inch casing accepts up to 148 mm OD. If the well has already been drilled and cased, confirm the actual casing ID before specifying pump OD — undersize nominal casings are common in older installations.

**Dewatering and drainage pumps** sacrifice head for flow. They have a wide-body open impeller or semi-open impeller design, can handle solids up to 25 mm in diameter, and are built for horizontal or near-horizontal installation at temporary sites. Construction site dewatering, mine drainage, pond management, and flood response are the primary use cases. Flow rates can reach 120 m³/h at heads of only 10–30 m. Because these are not permanently installed in a fixed well, IP54 or IP55 (splash-proof) is often the motor rating rather than full IP68 — confirm with the factory if continuous submersion is required.
