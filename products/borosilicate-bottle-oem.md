The key differentiator between borosilicate and soda-lime glass is the coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE). Borosilicate 3.3 has a CTE of approximately 3.3 × 10⁻⁶/K, compared to 8–9 × 10⁻⁶/K for standard soda-lime glass. Lower CTE means the glass expands and contracts less with temperature change, which is why borosilicate survives being filled with boiling water immediately after being at room temperature — a thermal differential of 80–100°C — without cracking. Our private label service helps you specify and verify material grades in your purchase orders, preventing cost-driven material substitution.

The standard thermal shock test for glass bottles is defined in ISO 7459 / EN 1186 equivalent procedures: fill with boiling water (100°C), hold for 5 minutes, then submerge in cold water (10°C). Borosilicate 3.3 passes this test; soda-lime glass typically fractures at differentials above 40–50°C.

Factories sometimes substitute soda-lime glass with a similar visual appearance to reduce cost. Field identification methods for buyers: borosilicate is slightly less transparent and has a faint yellow-green tint when viewed edge-on, whereas soda-lime glass has a green tint. More reliably, borosilicate has a density of approximately 2.23 g/cm³ vs. 2.5 g/cm³ for soda-lime — a weigh-and-measure check on a production sample will reveal substitution. Some factories use etched stamps or mold marks to indicate borosilicate grade; request a material test certificate (MTC) from the glass tube supplier, not just the factory's declaration.
