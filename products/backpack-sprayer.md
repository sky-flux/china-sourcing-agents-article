The choice between a **manual piston pump** and a **battery-powered diaphragm pump** determines the sprayer's application window, operator fatigue profile, and landed cost — not just the ex-factory unit price.

**Manual piston pump (lever-action).** The operator generates pressure on every stroke, typically delivering 0.2–0.4 MPa at the nozzle. There is no charging downtime, no battery management, and no electrical failure mode in the field. For plots <0.5 ha or for spot-treatment applications (weed control along fence lines, disease management on a small nursery block), manual sprayers remain the practical and cost-effective choice. The limitation is operator fatigue: sustained lever-action spraying over 2–3 hours causes arm and shoulder strain, which reduces spray quality as pressure becomes inconsistent. For high-volume daily use — orchards, large vineyards, row-crop herbicide applications — manual sprayers are the wrong tool regardless of cost.

**Battery-powered diaphragm pump.** A 12V motor drives a diaphragm pump that maintains consistent pressure without operator effort. The critical performance difference is spray uniformity: because pressure does not vary with stroke rhythm, droplet size distribution at the nozzle is more consistent, which directly affects coverage and chemical efficacy. Operator endurance over a full work shift improves significantly. A **12V 8Ah lithium pack** supports 4–8 hours of run time per charge depending on flow rate setting — plan for one spare battery on any site where downtime is costly.
