Silicone wire is specified by two independent variables that buyers frequently conflate: **stranding class** (which determines flexibility and fatigue life) and **insulation compound** (which determines temperature ceiling and chemical resistance). Confusing them leads to the wrong product on the BOM.

**IEC 60228 Class 5 vs Class 6.** Class 5 is fine-strand construction — typically 60–80 individual copper strands for a common AWG 22 hook-up wire. This gives enough flexibility for panel wiring and internal harnesses where the wire bends during assembly but remains largely static in service. Class 6 is ultra-fine strand, typically 200–500 strands for the same AWG, with individual strand diameters below 0.08 mm. Class 6 is required for **robot cable** (continuous flexing applications, torsional motion), EV battery pack interconnects where vibration cycling exceeds 10 million cycles, and medical device wiring where patient movement is continuous. Expect a 25–40% price premium for Class 6 over Class 5 at equivalent gauge, reflecting the higher drawing cost and tighter QC on individual strand diameter uniformity.

**Tinned vs bare copper.** Bare copper offers marginally lower bulk resistance (about 1.7% lower at 20°C) and costs slightly less. Tinned copper — where each strand is electroplated with a thin tin layer before stranding — provides oxidation resistance at elevated temperature, improved solderability, and meaningfully better performance in high-humidity or marine environments. For silicone wire operating at 150°C and above, bare copper oxidizes measurably over thousands of hours, increasing contact resistance at terminations. Most aerospace and automotive harness specifications mandate tinned stranding; for general industrial and PCB assembly hook-up wire below 100°C, bare copper is acceptable and economical.
