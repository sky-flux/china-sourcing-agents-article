The three dominant print methods for ceramic mugs differ in durability, cost, and regulatory footprint. Our private label service helps you evaluate the right print method for your price point and volume, and manages the entire branding process from artwork approval to production sampling.

**Sublimation** is the most common for photo-quality and full-wrap designs. The dye is transferred onto a polymer coating (usually polyester-based) applied over the glaze surface. Because the dye never penetrates the ceramic or glaze body, it is not directly food-contact — the underlying glaze is. However, sublimation coatings can degrade after 500+ dishwasher cycles, causing the image to fade or the coating to flake. When specifying sublimation mugs, request a dishwasher durability test per ASTM C1027 (decorative resistance to dishwasher damage). Firing temperature in sublimation processes is low (180–210°C in the transfer press), so the ceramic itself undergoes no additional glaze-sealing step.

**Reactive glaze** printing embeds metal oxide colorants directly into the glaze, which fuses to the ceramic at kiln temperatures of 1,200–1,280°C. The result is a permanent, food-safe decoration with no surface layer to degrade. Color accuracy is lower than sublimation — reactive glazes produce organic, variable patterns rather than photographic reproduction. Lead and cadmium are common in reactive glaze pigments, which is why independent heavy metal leaching tests are mandatory for these mugs (see below).
