A-grade lithium cells (Samsung 18650/21700, Panasonic, Murata, or CATL prismatic) maintain rated capacity for 300–500 charge cycles with less than 20% degradation. B-grade cells — commonly used in budget promotional power banks — are cells that failed quality grading at the battery factory. They may test at rated capacity when new but degrade to 60–70% capacity within 3–6 months of use. A corporate client who hands out 500 power banks and receives complaints that they "stopped working" within a year will not reorder. Our sourcing service identifies factories that can document cell provenance and provide cycle-life test data from the cell manufacturer.

Rated capacity is not the same as usable capacity: a 10,000 mAh power bank with B-grade cells and an inefficient DC-DC converter may deliver only 5,500 mAh to a connected device (55% efficiency). A quality unit with A-grade cells and a well-designed converter delivers 7,000–7,500 mAh at the output (70–75% efficiency). Request a capacity discharge test during sample inspection: fully charge the unit, then discharge through a 5V/2A dummy load and measure the actual mAh delivered.

UN 38.3 certification is the international standard for lithium battery transport — mandatory for air shipment. Power banks used as promotional gifts are frequently shipped by air courier (DHL, FedEx) to corporate clients. Without UN 38.3 documentation, courier companies can reject shipments or levy surcharges. Our sourcing service verifies that the UN 38.3 test report covers the exact cell configuration in your power bank — not just a generic cell certificate. For quality and capacity verification, our inspection service can perform discharge testing on production samples to confirm A-grade cell performance.
