ASTM F963 (US Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act toy standard) and EN 71 are largely aligned but not identical. Key differences: ASTM F963 Section 4.14 (small parts) uses a slightly different small parts cylinder test than EN 71; ASTM F963 Section 4.3 requires a specific flammability test method vs EN 71-2. For wholesale buyers targeting both markets, testing to both standards is necessary — combined testing adds approximately $800–1,200 per product but is a one-time cost. Our audit service can verify the factory's existing test certifications and identify gaps before you pay for duplicate testing.

The small parts cylinder test determines choking hazard classification: any piece that fits entirely within a 31.7mm diameter × 57.1mm deep cylinder is classified as a small part. For sets labeled "3+" years, all individual bricks must be too large to fit the cylinder. Verify this during sample inspection — some 1×1 round studs on cheaper sets are borderline. CA Prop 65 requires specific warning labels if any chemical in the product (including phthalates in ABS colorants) exceeds safe harbor thresholds.

EN 71-3 chemical safety covers migration of 19 elements from toy materials. For ABS plastics, the primary concern is phthalate plasticizers (used in some colorants to maintain flexibility) — DEHP, DBP, BBP are restricted to <0.1% by weight in toy materials per EU REACH Annex XVII. Request an EN 71-3 test report specifically covering the colored plastic materials, not just the uncolored base resin. Our sourcing service can coordinate third-party EN 71-3 and phthalate migration testing through accredited labs before you commit to production tooling.
