A branded power bank is a portable lithium battery pack customized with a corporate logo, Pantone-matched housing, or gift packaging and distributed as a promotional item, conference giveaway, or employee onboarding gift. Unlike retail power banks sold under a consumer brand, these units are built to a buyer’s brief: logo position, capacity, output configuration, and color. The typical order runs 200–2,000 units for events, with higher-end aluminum alloy versions reserved for executive gifts and lower-cost ABS units for trade-show handouts. When sourced well, a promotional power bank stays on a recipient’s desk for months, giving the brand repeated exposure; when sourced poorly, it becomes a fire hazard or a dead battery that damages the brand it was meant to promote. Because the product falls under consumer electronics, the same supply-chain discipline applied to mobile accessories — documented cell provenance, certified chargers, and pre-shipment testing — applies here.

A-grade lithium cells (such as Samsung 18650/21700, Panasonic, Murata, or CATL prismatic) are engineered to maintain their rated capacity for 300–500 full charge cycles with less than 20% overall degradation. Conversely, B-grade cells — commonly found in budget promotional power banks — are rejected cells that failed stringent quality grading at the battery factory. While they may test at their rated capacity when brand new, they typically degrade rapidly to 60–70% capacity within 3–6 months of regular use. A corporate client who distributes 500 branded power banks as corporate gifts and receives complaints that they "stopped working" within a year will not place a reorder. Our sourcing service identifies and vets OEM factories that can document verifiable cell provenance and provide authentic cycle-life test data directly from the cell manufacturer.

It's crucial to understand that rated capacity is not the same as usable capacity: cell capacity is rated at 3.7 V, so a 10,000 mAh pack holds about 37 Wh, and after the 5 V boost-converter and protection-circuit losses a usable 7,000–7,500 mAh at the output (70–75% conversion efficiency) is the realistic ceiling for a well-built unit. The IEC 62133-2 and IEEE 1725 frameworks both rate lithium packs at this 3.7 V nominal basis, so demand the discharge test be run that way. Always request a rigorous capacity discharge test during your sample inspection phase: fully charge the power bank, then discharge it completely through a 5V/2A constant-current load, log the energy delivered, and reject any sample whose usable output falls below 60% of the printed mAh.
